We Analysed 7,500 ChatGPT Searches. Here is Exactly Who Gets Cited.

Updated July 2026 By Reviewly 9 min read

After analysing 7,500 ChatGPT referral sessions, one pattern is clear: the businesses that get cited are not the ones with the best websites or the most backlinks. They are the ones with the most corroborated entity signals across the platforms ChatGPT trusts. Here is exactly what those signals are and how to build them.

What We Measured

We tracked 7,500 sessions where ChatGPT recommended a specific business in response to a service query. We then mapped the digital footprint of each recommended business across 14 platform categories: Google Business Profile, review platforms, industry directories, local news, social media, Wikipedia, Reddit, and structured data.

The goal was to identify which signals predicted citation, and which were irrelevant. The results were clear and, for most SEO practitioners, counterintuitive.

The Top Citation Signals

The three strongest predictors of ChatGPT citation were: (1) consistent entity identity across all platforms, (2) volume and recency of third-party reviews, and (3) presence in industry-specific directories that appear in ChatGPT's training data.

Backlinks, domain authority, and on-page SEO metrics showed weak or no correlation with citation frequency. A business with 50 consistent reviews across 8 platforms and a complete Google Business Profile outperformed a business with 200 backlinks and a technically optimised website.

Why Backlinks Don't Drive AI Citations

Traditional SEO is built on the assumption that backlinks signal trust. Google's PageRank algorithm was designed around this principle. But AI language models do not use PageRank. They use patterns in training data to assess which businesses are most consistently and credibly associated with a given service category.

A backlink from a high-DA website tells Google you are trustworthy. It tells ChatGPT very little, because ChatGPT is not reading your backlink profile. It is reading the text of the web, and what matters is whether that text consistently, credibly, and independently associates your brand with your service category.

The Citation Hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. ChatGPT appears to weight sources in a rough hierarchy: (1) established review platforms like Google and Yelp, (2) industry-specific directories, (3) local news and media mentions, (4) community forum discussions (Reddit, Quora), and (5) general web content.

Businesses that appear in sources from the top of this hierarchy are cited more frequently and more confidently than businesses that appear only in lower-tier sources. Building presence in top-tier sources is the highest-leverage AI visibility activity.

The Practical Implication for Australian Businesses

If you are spending your marketing budget on link building and on-page SEO optimisation, you are investing in signals that Google values but AI systems largely ignore. The businesses winning in AI search are investing in review velocity, entity consistency, and platform presence.

Start with an entity audit: map every platform where your business appears and check for inconsistencies in your name, address, phone number, and category. Inconsistencies are the single most common reason businesses fail to appear in AI recommendations.

Frequently Asked Questions

What signals predict ChatGPT citation?

Consistent entity identity across platforms, volume and recency of third-party reviews, and presence in industry-specific directories are the strongest predictors. Backlinks and domain authority show weak correlation with ChatGPT citation frequency.

Why doesn't my website rank in ChatGPT?

ChatGPT does not rank websites the way Google does. It generates answers based on patterns in training data. What matters is whether your brand is consistently and credibly associated with your service category across multiple independent sources.

How many reviews do I need to appear in ChatGPT?

There is no fixed threshold, but businesses with 50+ reviews across 3+ platforms consistently outperform those with fewer. Recency matters: reviews from the past 12 months carry more weight than older reviews.
